History & Culture

Jeju Island, known locally as Jejudo, is a volcanic island rich in both natural beauty and cultural depth. Formed through volcanic activity millions of years ago, the island’s dramatic landscapes—such as lava tubes, craters, and waterfalls—have shaped not only its terrain but also the way of life of its people. Historically, Jeju has been somewhat isolated from mainland Korea, allowing a unique culture to flourish. One of the most iconic cultural features is the Haenyeo, a centuries-old tradition of free-diving women who harvest seafood without modern equipment. These women represent the strength and resilience of Jeju’s matriarchal heritage. Visitors can also explore traditional thatched-roof stone houses, bustling local markets, and hear the Jeju dialect—elements that reflect a living culture rooted in nature and community.

Cultural Significance

Due to its geographical isolation, Jeju has nurtured a cultural identity distinct from the Korean mainland. This uniqueness is evident in its folklore, customs, and regional cuisine, such as the famous black pork barbecue and the abundant use of seafood. The island is dotted with Dol Hareubang, large volcanic stone statues believed to offer protection and fertility, which serve as enduring symbols of Jeju’s spiritual traditions. Seasonal festivals like the Fire Festival and Tamnaguk Ipchun Gutnori celebrate the agricultural calendar and ancestral beliefs. Jeju’s culture reflects not only adaptation to a harsh natural environment but also a deep respect for harmony between people and nature—a theme that continues to resonate with both locals and visitors today.
